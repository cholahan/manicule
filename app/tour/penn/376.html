4. The Form of the Maxim.

In the Grandison section on “Poets,” Richardson even adds a maxim not present in the original novel:

“Poets have finer imaginations that other men; but imagination and judgement seldom go together,

Poets, when provoked, are the wasps and hornets of society.”

The second part of the quotation, which is printed italics, was not taken from the original novel (see v.3, pp. 59). It also does not appear to be a common maxim from this period, suggesting it was likely written by Richardson himself while compiling Moral Sentiments. The metaphorical use of “wasps and hornets,” however, would have likely be familiar to many readers. A famous maxim written by Jonathan Swift procalimed:

“Laws are like cobwebs, which may catch small flies, but let wasps and hornets break through.”

The phrase is taken from Swift’s “A Tritical Essay Upon the Faculties of the Mind” (1707). It was later reprinted in Samuel Johnson’s Dictionary under “Cobwebs” and became a popular proverb in England and America.

We can conclude, then, that one of Richardson’s goals in creating Moral Sentiments was creating these types of maxims: quotations that would be frequently copied, in manuscript and printed form, travelling across different texts and even different continents.
