5. Beyond the Maxim

The Clarissa section is, especially, filled with these negative examples. Rather than exclude Lovelace from Moral Sentiments, Richardson uses Lovelace as negative example par excellence: not just as a caution for men, but also as a warning for women readers. On this page, for example, under “Advice and Cautions to Women,” the following maxim is included:

“I love, when I dig a pit, says Lovelace, to have my prey tumble in with secure feet and open eyes; for then a man can look down upon her with an O-ho, charmer! how came you there?” iii.25 [168].

Lovelace becomes a source of knowledge for women, to an even greater extent than he acts a negative example for men. By reading Lovelace’s thoughts and understanding his motivations, Richardson’s female readers can better understand libertines like Lovelace in their own lives — and thus better understand how to avoid men like him.

We can see a similar example on page 92, in which Lovelace is characterized as a prime example of the “true Libertine spirit”:

“It would be as unpardonable in a Lady, says Lovelace (in the true Libertine spirit) to break her word with me, as it would be strange, if I kept mine to her. In Love cases I mean; for as to rest, I am an honest moral man, vi. 317. [vii. 244].

These quotations of Lovelace offer the specific language and arguments that libertines might use, supposedly allowing female readers to become familiar with the arguments so that they can recognize them in real-life encounters.
