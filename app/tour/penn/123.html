3. Linking back to the novels

In certain cases, the book references these passages because it is the novelistic scene itself -- its poetic language, dialogue, and behavior of the character -- that offer a moral model. On this page, under “Death. Dying,” the reader is told to:

“See Clarissa’s noble behavior in the agonies of Death,” vii. 85 to 90. [viii. 3-8].

The reader who consults the section on death, perhaps expecting to find a few memorable maxims, is instead redirected to read a six page letter and make their own interpretation and meaning. On the other hand, it also enables the book to act as a jumping-point for re-reading specific moments of the novel. A reader who wants to return to the scene of Clarissa’s death -- perhaps to silently reread to themselves, or read aloud to family members or a friend -- can quickly consult Moral Sentiments to find its exact location.

Moral Sentiments, in short, offers multiple ways for the reader to utilize it, and multiple strategies by which to return, directly or indirectly, to the original novels.
