3. Linking back to the novels

In some cases, Moral Sentiments seems to references longer passages because it is the novelistic scene itself — its poetic language, dialogue, or the conduct of the character — that provides the moral lesson. In other words, these references imply that the moral lesson cannont be extracted or summarized from its larger novelistic context.

On this page, for example, under “Death. Dying,” the reader is told to:

“See Clarissa’s noble behavior in the agonies of Death,” vii. 85 to 90. [viii. 3-8].

The reader who consults the section on death, perhaps expecting to find a few memorable maxims, is instead redirected to read a six page letter and make their own interpretation and meaning.

These references also enable the book to act as a jumping-off point for re-reading the novel. A reader who wants to return to the scene of Clarissa’s death — perhaps to silently reread to themselves after a friend's passing, or to read aloud to a friend who has never read Clarissa — can quickly consult Moral Sentiments to find its exact location.

Moral Sentiments, then, offers multiple ways for the reader to utilize it, and multiple strategies by which to return — directly or indirectly — to the original novels.
