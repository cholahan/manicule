4. The Form of the Maxim.

On this page, in quotations under “Miscellaneous Observations,” we find the sentiment:

“The eye and the ear are too often great misleaders”

The quotation is extracted from the second volume of Grandison, on pp. 130, from a passage that reads:

“His good-sense and his politeness, and the pride he took to be thought one of the best-bred men in England, secured her complaisant treatment. But Lady Grandison had qualities that deserved one of the best and tenderest of men. Her eye and her ear had certainly misled her.”

Richardson shortens the observation into a more quotable and memorable statement; in doing so, however, the maxim might also become less clear in its moral lesson on vanity, a lesson that is so succinctly demonstrated by Lady Grandison’s actions.
