1. Introduction

In offering moral wisdom, then, Moral Sentiments likened itself to a secular extension of Biblical teachings. It was clearly a strategic choice, after all, for Richardson to ask Benjamin Kennicott, a biblical scholar, to write the preface.

Here, in the preface, Kennicott discusses the failure of the Gospel alone to cultivate virtue, writing,

“And yet, whatever improvements have been made by Christians in the system of Moral Duty; how powerfully soever men are now exhorted to act like reasonable and religious beings; how strongly soever those exhortations are enforced by the surer and more animating sanctions of the Gospel; yet—is it not too visible, that immorality and irreligion still obtain in the lvies of many, and, perhaps, (dreadful thought!) of the majority?”

Kennicott then goes on to say that many men, both of “clergy and laity,” have sought to written texts to stop “this alarming denegracy from growing more prevalent” — including Richardson, in Pamela, Clarissa, and Grandison. Richardson's Moral Sentiments, then, is framed as a lay work of morality that nevertheless builds on the Bible: attempting, we might even say, to further the work of the Bible where the Gospel has been ineffective. The texts seems especially targeted at young women (as evident by the sections on Advice to young married Women and Advice to Young Women in Pamela and on “Advice and Cautions to Women” in both Clarissa and Grandison) and, to a lesser extent, at young men (especially in the section from Sir Charles Grandison).
