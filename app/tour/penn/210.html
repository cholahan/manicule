5. Beyond the Maxim

There is evidence, however, that Richardson was anxious about his readers being able to identify which quotations in his Moral Sentiments offered positive models and which were being used as negative examples. At the top of the section “Reflections on Women”, a note is included on how the section should be read:

“Designed principally to incite Caution, and inspire Prudence, &c. by letting them know what Libertines and free Speakers say and think of the Sex”.


On this page, in this section on "Repentance" in Clarissa, the editor includes a declaration between quotations: “Lovelace lived not to repent!”



This declaration clarifies an early quotation, in which Lovelace claims that he wants “one day to be a reformed man,” and the one immediately after, in which Lovelace claims he has repented “a thousand times,” both found on page 396. It is likely that, as the editor, Richardson included this declaration to drive a "correct" reading of the character of Lovelace. By printing the declaration in a different font, Richardson calls attention to himself as editor and curator of not only this book, but of the reader’s reading experience in general: his work in the print shop, working with the physical books, adds editorial authority to his authorial one. His choice of a Blackletter typeface—which was used to print the Gutenberg Bible and was most common in the 15th and 16th centuries—might signal more 'antiquated' knowledge and can thus be read as an added assertion of editorial authority.
