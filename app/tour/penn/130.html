2. Organizational Structure

The division of Moral Sentiments by novel also suggests that Richardson believed he could not fully separate the moral lessons from their novel contexts — despite his best effort to do so in this work. To read the sentiments from Clarissa, for example, one would need to be at least cursorily aware of the novel's plots and characters to know that Lovelace is a bad model of character and thus to know how to read the sentiments that reference him.

It is possible that he also imagined the readers of Moral Sentiments to turn to specific novels for specific advice. Many of the topics tend to reflect the novels from which they are ‘extracted’. For example, the section on “Duty” from Clarissa here discusses, for the most part, the duty of children to their parents. The section on “Duty” from Pamela, in contrast, discusses a servant’s duty to their superiors (or, at times, lack thereof). This suggests that Richardson imagined that the readers of Moral Sentiments were already intimately familiar with his novels — they would know which novel to turn to for advice on different forms of duty.
