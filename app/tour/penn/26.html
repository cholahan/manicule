3. Linking back to the novels

The web of connections between Moral Sentiments and Richardson’s novels can at times extended beyond those parameters, when the novel refrences are refering, themselves, to external texts. On this page, for example, we find the maxim:

“No part of this superiority will be hereby lost, says Mr Locke; but the distinction increased, and their authority strengthened, when love in inferiors is joined with outward respect, and an esteem of the person has a share in their submission,” iv. 360. [314].

If we return to the referenced page in Pamela, we find a footnote redirecting us to page 298 of Locke’s “Thoughts Concerning Education.” These moment magnify the networked feeling of Moral Sentiments: at almost every step, the reader is redireted elsewhere, at times even allowing them to find multiple layers within a single maxim. This maxim, for example, can be read in isolation, or can be read in the context of a specific part of Pamela, or even in the context of Locke's original text.

See also the maxim on Health in Clarissa on page 135 of Moral Sentiments, in which Richardson quotes Joseph Addison (though employing his words on liberty to instead comment on health):

“What Mr. Addison says of Liberty, may, with still greater propriety, be said of Health; for what is Liberty itself without Health?
It makes the gloomy face of Nature gay;
Gives beauty to the Sun, and pleasure to the Day.”




The last two lines are also credited to Addison in the original passage from Clarissa on page 30 of volume six. The lines are taken from Addison's poem “To the Right Honourable the Lord Halifax, an earlier draft of Addison's A Letter from Italy (1704). The original lines read:

“O Liberty— [...]
Thou mak'st the gloomy face of Nature gay,
Giv'st beauty to the sun, & pleasure to the day”
