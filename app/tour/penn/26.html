3. Linking back to the novels

This web of connections between Moral Sentiments and Richardson’s novels is at times extended further, when the novel itself refers to external texts. On this page, for example, we find the maxim:

“No part of this superiority will be hereby lost, says Mr Locke; but the distinction increased, and their authority strengthened, when love in inferiors is joined with outward respect, and an esteem of the person has a share in their submission,” iv. 360. [314].

If we return to the referenced page in Pamela, we find a footnote redirecting us to page 298 of Locke’s “Thoughts Concerning Education.” These moment magnify the networked feeling of Moral Sentiments: at each step, the reader is referenced elsewhere, allowing them to find multiple layers to a single maxim. This maxim, for example, is read not only in isolation, but can be read in the context of a specific part of Pamela and in the context of Locke's original text.
