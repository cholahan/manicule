2. Organizational Structure

Richardson's project is thus, at its core, also an indexing project. Each "sentiment" refers back to a page in the original novel, thus constituting an index to those novels: directing how a reader might recollect, return to, and reread each novel. The Collection is also indexed itself, divided by novel. You can find a hyperlinked, digital version of the book's index here.

Moral Sentiments was certainly not Richardson's only or first foray into indexing. In 1751, Richardson included a collection of sentiments appended to Clarissa. In 1753, Richardson included two appendices to an edition of Sir Charles Grandison. The first was an "Index, Historical and Characteristical, to the six volumes of this work". Unlike Moral Sentiments, the index does not reproduce maxims, summaries, or quotes, but rather gives page number references for both topics (such as "Anger") and literary characters (such as "Anderson, Captain"). Richardson also includes a much shorter index of "Similes, and Allusions, in the foregoing six volumes", which refer to (rather than reproduce) maxims and literary phrases within the novel, such as "Learned Woman, To an owl among the birds" (408). Learn more about Richardson's earlier commonplacing efforts here)

Richardson's interest with indexing might suggest a wish to elevate the novel genre at a time when it was often considered an inferior literary production. Janine Barchas claims in Graphic Design, Print Culture, and the Eighteenth-Century Novel (2003) that eighteenth century indexing, “especially pioneered in fiction by Samuel Richardson, signaled the genre’s literary gravitas” (174).
