2. Organizational Structure

Richardson's project is thus, at its core, an indexing project. The Collection contains a detailed index, divided by novel. You can find a hyperlinked, digital version of the book's index here. The entire project of Moral Sentiments, however, is also an index of the original novels. Each "sentiment" refers back to a volume and page in the original novel, thus constituting an index to those novels: directing how a reader might recollect, return to, and reread each novel.

Moral Sentiments was certainly not Richardson's only or first foray into indexing. In 1751, Richardson included a collection of sentiments appended to Clarissa. In 1753, Richardson included two appendices to an edition of Sir Charles Grandison. The first appendix is titled an "Index, Historical and Characteristical, to the six volumes of this work." Unlike Moral Sentiments, the index does not reproduce quotations or offer summaries, but rather only gives the page number references for its topics (such as "Anger") and literary characters (such as "Anderson, Captain"). Richardson also includes a much shorter index of "Similes, and Allusions, in the foregoing six volumes," which refer to (rather than reproduce) maxims and literary phrases within the novel, such as "Learned Woman, To an owl among the birds" (408). Learn more about these earlier commonplacing efforts in 1751 and 1753 here.

Richardson's interest with indexing might suggest a wish to elevate the novel genre at a time when it was often considered an inferior literary production to poetry and drama. As Janine Barchas claims in Graphic Design, Print Culture, and the Eighteenth-Century Novel (2003), eighteenth century indexing, “especially pioneered in fiction by Samuel Richardson, signaled the genre’s literary gravitas” to its readers (174).
