2. Organizational Structure

Richardson's project is, at its core, also an indexing project. Each "sentiment" refers back to a page in the original novel, thus constituting an index to those novels: directing how a reader might recollect, return to, and reread each novel. The Collection is also indexed itself, which which has been reproduced with hyperlinks here.

Moral Sentiments was certainly not Richardson's only foray into indexing. In Sir Charles Grandison (1753), Richardson included an Index, Historical and Characteristical in the sixth and final volume. Unlike Moral Sentiments, the index does not reproduce maxims, sumamries, or quotes, but rather gives page numbers for both topics (such as "Anger") and characters (such as "Anderson, Captain"). However, Richardson also includes a much shorter index of "Similes, and Allusions, in the Foregoing Six Volumes" which refer to more maxim-like statements, such as "Learned Woman, To an owl among the birds" (here).

Richardson's interest with indexing might suggest a wish to elevate the novel genre at a time when it was often considered an inferior literary production. Janine Barchas claims in Graphic Design, Print Culture, and the Eighteenth-Century Novel (2003) that eighteenth century indexing, “especially pioneered in fiction by Samuel Richardson, signaled the genre’s literary gravitas” (174).
