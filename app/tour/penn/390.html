5. Beyond the Maxim

In addition to offering knowledgeable maxims and models of good and bad behavior, Moral Sentiments attempts to teach its readers how to read, and interpret, language.

Just as Richardson offers his readers libertine rhetoric as a way for them to recognize and avoid libertines, he offers “Signs of Love” in Sir Charles Grandison. The section offers ways for its reader to identify a man or woman in love (such as “sparkl[ing] eyes” and “glow[ing] cheeks”), as well as for a reader to identify love within themselves. For example, Moral Sentiments offers the following advice for its female readers:

“When a young woman finds both pleasure and pain in sighing, she should look to her heart,” iv. 63. [235]. (379).

Moral Sentiments, then, teaches readers to identify language and other clues within the novel as a way to teach them how to read outside the novel.
