3. Linking back to the novels

“The Vows of a maiden may be dispensed with by her Father when he hears them, Num XXX. 3, 4, 5. ibid.

This maxim is taken from a footnote of Clarissa, which can be seen here. Both the footnote and sentiment reference the original ‘source’ of the maxim, the Book of Numbers in the Bible:

“If a woman also vow a vow unto the Lord, and bind herself by a bond, being in her father’s house in her youth;

And her father hear her vow, and her bond wherewith she hath bound her soul, and her father shall hold his peace at her: then all her vows shall stand, and every bond wherewith she hath bound her soul shall stand.”

But if her father disallow her in the day that he heareth; not any of her vows, or of her bonds wherewith she hath bound her soul, shall stand: and the Lord shall forgive her, because her father disallowed her.”

- Numbers 30:3-5. As quoted from the King James Bible.

This is the rare case in which Richardson included a Biblical verse in the Moral Sentiments. The fact that the Bible verse was originally referenced in a footnote, rather than the main text, suggests that he imagines the reference acting as a type of reinforcement: the reader who might be tempted to disagree with Richardson’s claim is told to do so would be to also disagree with scripture.

The scripture reference also reminds us that eighteenth-century readers were expected to constantly reference, return to, and re-read the Bible. In fact, we might say that Moral Sentiments frames Richardson’s original novels as similar to Scripture: Richardson acts his readers to treat the novels as authoritative material that should be constantly referenced and re-read.
