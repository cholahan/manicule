4. The Form of the Maxim.

However, all of the novel passages are split into shorter sentiments, in some cases the sentiments only make sense when read linearly. On this page under “Single Women,” for example, we are given the following sentiments:

“The longer a woman remains single, the more apprehensive she will be of entering into the state of wedlock,

At seventeen or eighteen, a girl will plunge into it, often without either fear or wit,

At twenty, she will begin to think,

At twenty-four, she will weigh and discriminate,

At twenty-eight, she will be afraid of venturing,

At thirty, will turn about, and look down the hill she has ascended, and sometimes rejoice, sometimes repent, that she has gained that summit, sola (v.4, pg. 140)

If a reader were to flip to this page and randomly pick the third line as an individual sentiment, they would be confused. We cannot read and understand the fragment “At twenty, she will begin to think” without reading the earlier lines. What, then, is achieved by splitting up these passages?

First, the chopping up of prose constantly reminds the reader that they can stop reading at any moment, shifting to a later sentiment about “Single Women,” a different subject altogether, or back to the novel. This visual effect of the split quotations thus emphasises and teaches a form of non-linear reading.

Secondly, the splitting up of passages allows the reader to see the passage as a group of smaller components that might be picked out and rearranged. A reader copying passages into their commonplace book, or in a letter to a friend, might simply copy the first sentiment (“The longer a woman remains single…”) and only one of the subsequent sentiments, such as (“At thirty…”), allowing them to create, through selection and rearrangement, a moral sentiment specifically about women in their thirties. We might say, then, that Richardson's Moral Sentiments also teaches the art of commonplacing.
